Does Gal√°pagos marine iguanas shrink in size to survive El Ni√±o events?

Marine iguanas from the Gal√°pagos might actually shrink during low food periods caused by El Ni√±o events. The Iguanas data contain the change in body size (change.in.length) for 64 Gal√°pagos marine iguanas (Amblyrhynchus cristatus) that survived the 1992-1993 El Ni√±o event. Conduct a statistical test to determine if the iguanas shrank in size during the El Ni√±o event.

Exercise 2.1.1

  • Plot the histogram of the change.in.length variable with 20 bins.

Solution

ggplot(Iguanas, aes(change.in.length)) + geom_histogram(bins = 30, color = "white") 
ggplot(Iguanas, aes(change.in.length)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 30, color = "white")

Exercise 2.1.2

  • Conduct a statistical test to determine if the iguanas shrank in size during the El Ni√±o event, assuming the change in length is approximately normally distributed.

Solution

______(Iguanas$change.in.length, mu = 0, alternative = "less") 
______(Iguanas$change.in.length, mu = 0, alternative = "less")

Exercise 2.1.3

  • Based on your answer to the previous question, what is the conclusion of the test using a significance level of 0.05?

Exercise 2.1.4

  • What is the 98% confidence interval for the mean change in length of the iguanas during the El Ni√±o event?

Solution

confint(t.test(Iguanas$change.in.length, alternative = "two.sided", conf.level = 0.98)) 
confint(t.test(Iguanas$change.in.length, alternative = "two.sided", conf.level = 0.98))

Does drinking beer attract more mosquito bites for humans?

Mosquitoes find their victims in part by odor, so what people consume many influence the attractiveness to mosquitos. A study in West Africa, opened a container holding 50 mosquitoes next to each of 25 alcohol-free participants and measured the proportion of mosquitoes that left the container and flew toward the participants (they called this proportion the ‚Äúactivation‚Äù). They repeated this procedure 15 minutes after each of the same participants had consumed a liter of beer and measured the ‚Äúchange in activation‚Äù (after minus before). This procedure was also carried out on another 18 human participants who were given water instead of beer. The change in activation of mosquitoes is given for both the beer-and water-drinking groups.

Exercise 2.2.1

  • Draw a boxplot, superimposed with the stripchart, for each group to compare the change in activation of mosquitoes.

Solution

Since there are not too many observations per group, stripchart helps us to see the distribution of the individual data points. Boxplot depicts a five number of summary statistics (minimum, first quartile, median, third quartile, and maximum) and can sometimes mask subtle details in the data.

ggplot(mosquito, aes(group, change)) + geom_boxplot() + geom_jitter(width = 0.05) 
ggplot(mosquito, aes(group, change)) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  geom_jitter(width = 0.05)

Exercise 2.2.2

  • Is there evidence to suggest that drinking beer attracts more mosquito bites than drinking water? Assuming that the change in activation of mosquitoes is approximately normally distributed, conduct a statistical test to answer this question.

Solution

t.test(beer, water, alternative = "greater") 
t.test(beer, water, alternative = "greater")

Exercise 2.2.3

  • Based on your answer to the previous question, what is the conclusion of the test using a significance level of 0.01?

Is this pesticide claim true?

A farmer is testing a new pesticide with claims that it is more effective than the standard pesticide, which successfully protects 70% of crops from insect damage. To test this, the farmer applies the new pesticide to 40 plants and observes that 32 of them remain undamaged.

Exercise 2.3.1

  • Assuming that plants are homogeneous and no other factors are involved, conduct a statistical test to determine if the new pesticide is more effective than the standard one.

Solution

binom.test(x = 32, n = 40, p = 0.7, alternative = "greater") 
binom.test(x = 32, n = 40, p = 0.7, alternative = "greater")
